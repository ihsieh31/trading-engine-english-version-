# The New York Stock Exchange (1905)

A historical account of the NYSE's founding, evolution, rules, and practices through the early 20th century.

## Founding and Early History

### The Buttonwood Agreement (1792)
- 24 brokers signed under a buttonwood tree on Wall Street
- Agreed to trade only with each other at 0.25% commission
- Birth of organized securities trading in America

### The NYSE Charter
- Formal constitution adopted in 1817
- Moved into dedicated buildings over the years
- Developed rules for listing, trading, and settlement

## Market Structure (circa 1905)

### Participants
- **Specialists**: Assigned to specific stocks, maintained orderly markets
- **Floor Brokers**: Executed orders for member firms
- **Odd-Lot Dealers**: Bought/sold shares in less than 100-share lots
- **Two-Dollar Brokers**: Independent brokers executing for other members
- **Commission Houses**: Dealt with the public

### Trading Process
- Open outcry auction system on the floor
- Each stock traded at a designated post
- Specialists managed the order book and provided liquidity
- Continuous auction from 10 AM to 3 PM (then 3 PM close)
- Price determined by highest bid and lowest offer

## Key Instruments Traded

### Stocks
- Common shares (voting equity)
- Preferred shares (fixed dividend, priority in liquidation)
- Certificates of deposit
- Voting trusts

### Bonds
- Government bonds (federal, state, municipal)
- Railroad bonds (largest private bond category)
- Industrial bonds (growing segment)
- Convertible bonds

### Other
- Rights and warrants
- Stamps and coupons
- Gold certificates (historically)

## The Clearing House

### Purpose
- Net settlement between member firms
- Reduced physical certificate movement
- Lowered counterparty risk

### Process
- Daily netting of obligations
- Delivery of securities vs payment
- Failed delivery penalties

## Trading Rules and Ethics (Historical)

### Membership Requirements
- Purchase of a seat (limited to 1,100 members)
- Character and financial responsibility review
- Adherence to the NYSE constitution

### Prohibited Practices
- Wash sales (artificial trading)
- Cornering the market (controlling supply)
- Spreading false rumors
- Bucket shop operations (betting on prices without actual trade)

### Settlement
- Regular way: delivery next business day
- Cash: same day settlement
- Seller's/buyer's option: extended settlement

## Market Milestones Pre-1905

- **1830s**: First railroad stocks listed
- **1860s**: Civil War financing boom, gold trading
- **1873**: Panic of 1873 — market closed for 10 days
- **1880s**: Industrial stock listings surge
- **1893**: Panic of 1893 — railroad bankruptcies
- **1901**: Northern Pacific corner — stock reached $1,000

## Lessons for Modern Traders

### From the Past
- Market structure shapes behavior — understanding mechanics reveals opportunities
- Panics follow predictable patterns: overextension → shock → forced liquidation
- Regulation follows crisis — the NYSE's rulebook grew after every panic
- Seat prices reflect market confidence — a leading indicator

### Enduring Principles
- Settlement integrity is the foundation of market trust
- Liquidity providers (specialists) are essential in volatile times
- Market rules evolve, but human behavior repeats
- Transparency and standardized listing requirements attract capital

## Closing Quote
> "An exchange exists not for the benefit of its members, but for the public which entrusts its savings to the market. When this is forgotten, the exchange fails in its fundamental purpose."
