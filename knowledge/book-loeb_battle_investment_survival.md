# The Battle for Investment Survival (Loeb)

Gerald M. Loeb's classic (1935/1957) on capital preservation and the psychological battle of investing.

## Core Philosophy
- Investing is a battle — the goal is to "make a killing without being killed"
- Capital preservation comes first, profits second
- The market is a voting machine in the short run, a weighing machine in the long run
- "Put all your eggs in one basket, and watch that basket"

## Key Principles

### 1. Speculative Attitude is Essential
   - All investing involves speculation — accept this
   - The investor who ignores risk will lose everything
   - There is no such thing as a "safe" investment

### 2. Cut Losses, Ride Winners
   - The single most important rule: take small losses quickly
   - Let profits run until there's clear evidence the trend has changed
   - Most investors do the opposite: hold losers and sell winners early

### 3. Concentrate Holdings
   - Diversification dilutes returns
   - Put capital into your best ideas, not a broad basket
   - Watch your holdings carefully — concentration demands attention

### 4. Know When to Be Out
   - Cash is a position
   - Sitting out during bear markets is essential to survival
   - Being fully invested at all times is a fallacy

### 5. Price is What Matters
   - Fundamentals are irrelevant if price action disagrees
   - The tape tells the story — learn to read it
   - Never argue with the market

### 6. The Trend is Your Friend
   - Don't try to pick bottoms or tops
   - Buy into strength (confirmed uptrend), not weakness
   - Sell into weakness (confirmed downtrend), not strength

### 7. Use Limit Orders
   - Market orders reveal your hand
   - Patience in execution saves money
   - The difference between bid and ask is the market's profit, not yours

### 8. Avoid the Crowd
   - When everyone is bullish, the top is near
   - When everyone is bearish, the bottom is near
   - Independent thinking is the investor's greatest asset

## Battle Plan for Every Trade
1. Write down the single ruling reason for entering
2. Set a stop-loss before entering
3. Define profit target
4. Review the trade after exit — learn from both wins and losses
5. Keep records of every trade and the reasoning behind it

## Warning Signs
- Buying stocks because they are "cheap" (low price)
- Following tips or hot stories
- Averaging down on losing positions
- Selling winners to hold losers
- Overtrading — excessive commissions destroy returns

## Closing Quote
> "The stock market does not change from one era to another. The actual stocks change names, but the behavior of the market remains the same because human nature does not change."
